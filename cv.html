How to Write a CV 101

A curriculum vitae or CV is your opportunity to grab an employer's attention and get an interview. It showcases your qualifications, achievements and work experience.

Before you start

Read the job description

The job description shows you what the employer is looking for from an ideal candidate. Look for the key skills and think about how your own past experience relates to the job. This will help you tailor your CV to the position.

You will spot key words and get used to the language the employer is using. Use the same language when you create a CV, so it is easier for the recruiter reading it to see how you match up.

Think about the structure of your CV

When you write a CV, you can lay it out in different ways, depending on what you want to show the employer. Put the most important information for this particular job is at the top. Have they asked for a qualification? Do they want work experience in something? Or are there specific skills required? The job description will help you decide what to prioritise.

The things you need to have on your CV

Check everything off this list, before you send off your CV

1. Contact details

Make it easy for the hiring managers to get in touch. Include your address, main phone number and professional e-mail address.

2. Education and qualifications

Talk about skills and knowledge that you have developed and highlight any key achievements that relate to the job.

As an aspiring developer, you need to showcase your tech stack and the easiest way to do this is to list the stack under a previous project that you have worked on. Another way is to showcase your projects on GitHub.

3. Work experience

Highlight tasks and responsibilities that show your skills and strengths. Include those which are relevant to the role you are applying for.

If you have not had many, or any, work experience, think about other things you have been involved in such as university societies or volunteer work.

Tip: Use action verbs to demonstrate your experiences. Examples include: "applied", "improved", and "implemented". Click here for a more extensive list

4. References

To save space, you do not have to include references on your CV, but make sure you state that they are available on request. Remember to ask your referees if they are willing to provide you with a reference, so they will expect someone to be in touch.

Additional things to note

Layout

Try to keep to two sides of A4. Employers will scan your CV - mke it easy for them to spot key words by using bullet points and headings.

Spelling and punctuation

Avoid spelling mistakes. Use the spell check on your computer but do not rely on it fully to find every error. Ask someone with a good grasp of grammar and spelling to check your CV.

Styling

In general, avoid fancy fonts, borders, tables and graphics. They can be distracting. There are exceptions - if a styled CV shows your skills as a graphic or UX/UI designer, for example.

Honesty is the best policy

Be honest about your qualifications and experience. If you have lied on your CV, you could easily be caught out at the interview stage!
